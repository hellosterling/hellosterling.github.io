The ASAP Trap: Escaping Chaos Through Operational Triage

A case study on high-availability resource allocation and human change management.

What does all the high-level operational jargon actually mean in layman’s terms? Okay, let’s toggle off the AI-make-your-eyes-glaze-over button and talk like humans.

First, the high-availability resource allocation thing.

When I first started as the “Head of Content” in January 2021, I inherited a handful of freelance writers and was asked to find more. Looking back at my old Writer’s Roster spreadsheet, I see a total of 50 names on there (almost all of them crossed out).

Our CEO told me he liked to give writers around seven line items at a time with a note: “As soon as you get these back to me, I’ll have more for you.” His idea was that this should cause writers to turn in their work quickly so they could get another batch of seven articles to write.

However, his management style and mine were vastly different. This is not a dig on my boss--he is absolutely amazing and I'll bet he could single-handedly run our whole operation from his kitchen table if he wanted to go back to doing that. But as the CEO, he was not concerned about the same things I (as his brand new head of content) would be concerned about. I call his assignments method the Fast & Furious method.

Then he’d say to the writers, “G-chat me with questions!” (The freelance writers I have worked with are all pretty chat-averse, though.)

Because I am not the CEO of a startup but rather the person responsible for executing content assignments for the CEO, I have always tried to provide organized, cleanly formatted spreadsheets. After all, I demand organized, cleanly formatted content from the writers.

I also try to provide enough instructions so that the writer will not have to spend a great deal of time trying to figure out what we want from them before they start working. And I am also in the habit of providing quite a lot of feedback, as part of my desire is for freelancers to improve and grow.

The Legacy Workflow Loop

Initially, my workflow looked like this:

  • Create the assignment sheet/content docs. Update our master sheet with the writer’s details/fees.
  • Email the writer with a link to the spreadsheet and set a deadline. Mention that if he or she turned in the content early they could potentially get more–provided they didn’t lag too long in the month. (Our workflow was front-of-the-month-heavy.)
  • QA the finished content and write the feedback email. Rewrite my feedback over and over until I was pretty sure I wouldn’t hurt their feelings, because I know as a writer that even constructive criticism stings.
  • Ask if they wanted to write more articles this month.
  • Wait for their reply and then start the whole process all over again.

The Post-Pandemic Freelance Reality

Possibly due to the strange dynamics of the era immediately following the pandemic, nearly all freelancers who called themselves “content writers” and who were in our price range struggled to adapt and improve from feedback. Writers frequently told me, “Thanks, I’ll add this to my notes” and then made the same mistakes during the next batch.

Many also seemed to want uncomfortably long deadlines, which meant I could sometimes only give them one small batch per month. In fact, more writers than you might think frequently blew right past deadlines before sending an email like, “Sorry, one of my kids was sick this weekend, I’ll get those articles to you as soon as possible.” The weekend in question was often two weeks after they had received the assignment. Note that I said frequently.

Don’t get me started on the quality of the writing. This type of content isn’t expected to be great, but even link building departments need standards. When hiring, I required a writing sample. The writing samples were often great. Yet the quality of the articles they turned in were often much, much lower than the quality of their writing samples. So many writers only lasted one round, and instead of giving them feedback I just said, “Thank you, and best of luck in all your endeavors.”

Several people who managed a group of offshore writers even tried to sneak in as being “one writer.”

The Operational Fix: Consolidation & Triaging

The best solution ended up being this: Pick the writers who meet their deadlines the best and seem to be able to handle larger assignments. Ask them if they’d like even bigger assignments. Encourage them like crazy every time they completed the assignments on time.

In other words, giving all the work to a small group of “regulars” meant:

  • Fewer feedback emails
  • Fewer explanations
  • Faster QA’ing
  • “Triaging”

That last bit about triaging is invaluable. I have worked as a manager in extremely fast-paced environments and, frankly, “Hey can I get this ASAP” bums people out. Hearing it several times each month actually seems to take a legitimate toll on their overall quality of work and responsiveness.

However, if each person has an assignment spreadsheet where I keep adding assignments, I can just tell them up front, “I’ll occasionally change up the order of your spreadsheet due to various client needs. Would you mind just going in order from top to bottom?”

Then I can easily swap the order of rows as needed without ever having to say “ASAP.”

I can still hear it all the time, but the creatives don’t have to.
